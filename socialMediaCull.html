pitchforks and torches




those that had to go

Sorry bebo, you were my first but the love didn't last, as soon as Facebook came along you were always second best. Despite holding off joining the Facebook bandwagon as long as possible, when it got to the point where the only comment updates I got was from ladies 'wanting a good time' or trying to sell me an assortment of pills it was time to leave the sinking ship also. I have no interest in your app-only reboot, some things are better left in the past!

myspace, you are another one of my early favourites, but what happened? After putting up a bit of a fight against Facebook in the late naughties, it seems to be dieing a slower death that necessary. Should have/ could have included something along the lines of Spotify from the onset when it was THE cool music site. But, all too late now...

To be fair, Vemeo is making some strides as an alternative to Youtube. For me however, not enough to warrant an account. One less distraction in my bookmark bar.

I wanted Ello to work, I really did. I sent out recommendations to my friends religiously in attempt to get a base of 'real life' contacts as well as building up a collection of new, virtual friends. The virtual friends came, the real one's didn't. I think it's a cool idea, but there's too much of an overlap with Facebook to bring that many people onto Ello, which offers the same sort of set up but without ads and marketing. I hope Ello makes it, but at the moment I can't justify spending any more of my time on this type of platform.

Google + is Google's problem child in an otherwise unstoppable juggernaut of technologies. Some people think that companies such as Apple and Google have too much of an engineering mind to give the more creative social platform a good go. Perhaps they're right. With even Google admitting they don't have any plans for where their social platform is going to go, it's time to remove this one from my bookmarks bar - the account of course will remain, with so many other useful websites allowing access through it!

Pinterest is apparently now geared towards the male audience more so than ever before. None the less I can't see any reason for me to be active on this site. Good for creative ideas, also for assimilating copius amounts of time.

Not only as it's another time cruncher, Behance has been completely deleted out of protest to Adobe. They're very good at what they do, I am a big fan of Photoshop and InDesign in particular. However, their subscription only service for Adobe products has me with a bad taste in my mouth. As a hobby 'designer' or more appropriately 'player around with photos, etc' I can't justify paying the subscription fee each month for the rest of my life. If I was involved in Graphic Design on a full time basis, and I did genuinely see a reason to have somewhere online where I could showcase all my output from Adobe products then Behance would be it.

Tumbler was a site I never got into. Growing up I never had any desire to become a blogger, with Facebook and Twitter providing me with a more platform to communicate with the digital world. Today I have this website - no need for the Tumbler anymore.

those that nearly went

This one may surprise you but Facebook was on the hit list, it has in fact been in the line of fire on a number of occasions over the last few years. Productivity suffers because of it, and some people fill up my timeline with constant jibberish about their lives, which should really remain in the private sphere, or at least out of my daily down time. None the less, it remains. The benefits it provides for keeping in touch just (JUST) outweigh the negatives.

I was late on the Instagram bandwagon due to an early love of the original Windows 7 phone - which had such a bad camera that photos were completely out of the question. I feel as if I may have missed something with the initial Instagram surge, and don't get a lot out of it. The limited capability on the desktop site also causes me some discomfort, but overall I have no reason to remove this app from my phone. From time to time, I'll take the odd interesting photo and want to upload it somewhere to an audience who will appreciate it without taggin faces or adding a comment; Instagramers are the best choice for this type of audience.


those that survived, for now...

LinkedIn and Xing are my 'go-to' networks on a day-to-day basis as I look to continue to build a professional Network. Xing is the German version of LinkedIn, and the Germans (and Swiss and Austrians) seem to be giving it their full support. It offers more or less the same features, although not as many as LinkedIn for a free account. These platforms are a great way of connecting with new people on a professional level. There aren't many boozey pictures on my Facebook, but still at networking events I only mention my LinkedIn profile, it provides a much more professional contact platform with the added benefit of being able to follow people such as Richard Branson and many other experts in their respective professional fields. It remains a great source of inspiration and motivation, and tends to be career related information that actually helps my development.

Twitter has won me over in the last year. I initially had twitter back when it started in 2006, but lost interest after a number of months either due to my obsession with Facebook growing up or because of following the wrong people. I rejoined and left twitter a further 2 times, before joining one last time in late 2013 and haven't looked back since. This time I have tried to avoid adding my friends as much as possible (hear me out), as I have them all already on Facebook. I don't need to see the same message twice on 2 different feeds. This time I have concentrated on following interesting people who are stimulating for a number of different reasons. Some are Software developers like myself, other's simply live in cities that interest me, or share hobbies that I like to keep in touch with. Twitter is now for me the best of all the social media sites, I just hope they don't play around with the format too much, as I hear that financially the current model is not sustainable. Once money comes into play, I fear for how and what changes will be made to this great platform.

Finally, Flickr is my new photo sharing website. I always had a keen interest in photography, but only showed my photos to family or friends in person; either in constantly changing picture frames around the house or with albums and photobooks. Before I rejoined Flickr earlier this year, I didn't feel that there was an appropriate online platform for sharing photos with others. Of course I uploaded Facebook and Twitter pics, but the pictures I actually think of as 'Photography' I had kept at home, locally on my machine to throw up on screen every now and again when people were round. I think this has changed with my return to Flickr. I carefully select photos which I upload, and again I will avoid adding my Facebook friends as much as possible to concentrate on connecting with new individuals. Flickr for me is more of an output for creativity as a site for keeping in touch with friends. Keeping in mind this functionality, and avoiding getting too caught up in the networking side of things, I believe that I can use Flickr as a tool to connect with interesting people who also share a passion for photography.


Be sure to let me know your musings on twitter, keep me up-to-date on LinkedIn , or check out my photography 'skills' with Flickr.
