Work Experience


Architectural Engineer - Germany


Employed as an Architectural Technician, the firm specialised in chemical laboratory projects and is based right in the centre of the main chemical development region in Europe. This challenge provided me with many obstacles to overcome, as well as technical and chemical terms that many of the native population of Germany would at least have to think twice about, there was the added complications of such an industry with regards to health and safety whilst carrying out investigations with hazardous materials. Projects varied from development institutes, to University laboratories. Each project was very different, involving mainly aspects relating to Chemistry, Physics and Biology.

My responsibilities grew within a relatively short time to include liason with suppliers, to overcome space issues whilst maintaining an exceptionally high level of safety for the user. Then, I was able to go back to my desk and design a solution to the problem faced, before passing this information on to our representative on site or in some cases I had the opportunity of attending site meetings myself with the other consultants and relay this information myself.

I am grateful for the opportunities that I received here - because of these, I learned the more about how a German office works in this position than any other. I have thought about it many times, and people ask me frequently; how do the Germans work, what makes them so different from us? Unfortunately there is no simple answer. When your there, it seems that there is an unwritten procedure that everyone works to. I simply turned up and slotted into this procedure. Maybe it is to do with their society, which is much different than in the UK. They definitely don't have a magic recipe for it, their system simply works and works well.




Telecommunication Site Planning


Working as a self-employed contractor I took on a number of projects through a friend working with UK mobile providers as they replaced mobile masts and sites with newer technologies. The task was simple; a surveyor would send through photographs, a site survey and technical information on a site, and it was my job to create detailed CAD drawings from this information. CAD is one of those things I can start into and not notice as the day passes.

Self-employment takes a lot of self motivation. I had that, working sometimes between 8 and 14 hours a day when there was work on the table. What drove me to leave the business, is the periods in between when there is no work. It wasn't even about the money, it was simply being bored for hours, days and sometimes weeks on end whilst waiting for the next batch of drawings to come through. As a self declared sufferer of a higher than average level of OCD - this lack of routine got to me quite quickly.

Having said that I am still open to side projects involving similar work. Technical drawing is a skill that I would like to continue to develop. I look for small jobs on volunteering sites, or just odds and ends from contacts still in the industry - just something to get the mind going through the gears again.




Pratikant - Bonn


A short and sweet working as a placement student at a busy central Architecture office in Bonn, provided me with an insight into how the profession worked in Germany. I found it to be in some ways very similar but in others to be very very different.

I was tasked within this position to help a architect on a number of his projects. This involved firstly learning a new tool - Nemetschek Allplan. This is a German made piece of software, I saw it in the beginning as a cross between Google Sketchup and Autocad. I picked up the software within a week, and discovered that it is in fact a very powerful tool to have. I assume that I hadn't heard of it before as it, at the time, was only available in German. Very easy to use as a design tool, on the click of a button the user can have access to full 3D and detailed drawings. The software also provides functionality to price the components, and schedule the build, all with minimal extra effort.

Having got up to speed with the tools, I was given a number of survey tasks, some of which involving taking measurements for a refurbishment job and others visiting on site projects and documenting progress whilst speaking with the clients and contractors. I was only able to get a months experience here, but am pleased to say that my last project before I left was my most rewarding; a 3D model of a roof conversion and house extension that was to be used on presentation boards for the client. One which I delivered on time and to a high standard.

After gaining this experience, it became clear to me that Architecture in Germany was a lot different to what I had studied. I spoke with the architects working there, who suggested that if I wanted to continue my career as an architect in Germany, to go back to University and learn it the way that had learned. Otherwise they saw my studies as a possible hindrance to getting a permanent job. After consideration and as a result of consultation with 2 of the main Universities in the area, I decided not to go down this route.




Technical Specialist - Cologne


My first construction job in Germany - it was sold to me as a position representing the company to contractors and architects. The company is involved in private, hydraulic powered lifts, designed for home use but in some cases also used for small commercial and public buildings.

After 4 weeks of intense training, I was happy enough that I had made the right choice. I was competent in the technical aspects of the job, I had worked hard on getting the language up to scratch (German office language is somewhat different from day-to-day use), and I felt I was in a good position to get started in the construction industry.

The job however quickly turned out to be not at all what I had expected. More of a paper-pushing position, where I found myself more and more involved with the contractual side of projects and the management of installation teams and the ordering of custom parts. I quickly became unhappy in the position, and decided I had to leave as quickly as possible. I did get the chance to utilize my CAD skills once, and also was commended on my performance from a building contractor regarding my ideas for solving a major problem we had on site. Apart from these 2 instances, I can't really think of any rewarding experiences during my time here.

I think my experience in this job was down to my understanding of the position offered at the interview stage. What I saw and what they offered were apparently 2 separate things. From this point on I went into interviews with a lot more questions prepared regarding what the position actually entailed. At the end of the day I had spent time at a company where I could have perhaps developed more somewhere else. I didn't however leave completely empty handed, I got numerous lessons in the German office culture of being held accountable for your actions, unfortunately in my experience it was only when something hadn't gone to plan. None the less, I took it as a step in the right direction, and sought out my next challenge.

Kitchen Planner


Maybe not something everyone with an Architecture degree would have thought of, but I really enjoyed my time planning kitchens for one of Europes biggest home furnishing retailers. The idea behind kitchen planning is relatively straightforward, but the customers keep things interesting by throwing any number of unique problems to overcome.

Having worked in Retail since I left school and while I was studying in University, I thrived in a customer facing environment. The customer had their own ideas, I knew what could or could not be done, and it was the interaction involved in making the 2 match up that made this job so interesting.

Sometimes my architectural background did come in very useful, with the odd customer planning to take down a wall or relocate water services within their kitchen. All of which I could at least give them a bit of information about, and a few of my ideas as to what and how it could be done. The other benefit of this job was when the proud customers came back into visit and show off their new kitchens after installation. It gave me a lot of satisfaction to know that the job had been done so well, the customer was wanted to brag about it.


Architectural Assistant


One year's work placement and another year part time whilst I was finishing my degree was my time in the UK Construction industry. In that time I learned a lot form some great people. I struggled at the start with the office job, set hours where most people only moved from their desk to leave at the end of the day. Office jobs also require a completely different form of self motivation, one I still struggle with as I previously thrived a lot off the customer interaction that retail provides (even when dealing with a complaint).

Site surveys, outline design, planning, working and detail drawings as well as 3D modelling and rendering were all tasks I took on working with several others, over a wide range of high profile projects in the city. I had a lot of help in learning AutoCad and 3D modelling software such as 3DSMax. I also gained experience in different types of projects, including conservation, regeneration, refurbishment and new build.

The first project I was involved in was a Pharmaceutical Building. I worked as an assistant to the project architect, producing 3D models and proposed drawings for a new hospital building in one of Belfast's most established hospitals. I enjoyed the modeling work, and talking with the nurses and pharmacists who worked in the existing facility in order to meet their day to day needs with the new building. I carried out surveys and specifications.

I then gained experience on a longer running project, the refurbishment and extension of a listed museum building, which ran over five years. I started at the construction phase and worked with the contractors on site to provide them with any drawings they needed throughout the build. I was also involved in site meetings and surveys, to continually assist in the smooth running of the build. As the building was older than usual and listed there were a lot of challenges to deal with.

The third major project I was involved in was a cultural centre for arts and music in the heart of Belfast, and was another refurbishment and extension project. Working as an assistant to the project Architects, I was involved with production of presentation boards for client shows and meetings. This involved a lot of photoshop and CAD drawings in order to communicate effectively the ideas from the Architect to the client.

One of the few residential projects I was involved in was a residential houseing project. This was the first project where I was allowed more freedom. I was asked to create an outline proposal for a housing development that involved two new houses, in addition to an extension of the existing property.The plans were put through the local planning authority and accepted after minimal amendments, and the client was satisfied to have the plans ready to go, after they found a suitable developer for the project.

The final major project I was able to work on was a welfare building for minorities. There were a lot of people from different backgrounds that the design team had to work with. I worked with the project architect from initial design to the completion of the build, and was very excited and satisfied to work with a project to completion. This was another project I used as part of my university dissertation. It involved consideration of the sustainability issues of a project in a built up urban area with many environmental concerns.




about

Architectural Technology is all about the details... From understanding the project requirements, to the behaviours of building materials and the effective use of space,I am still thoroughly interested in good Architecture. During my placement, I had the opportunity to work on a number of highly interesting and unique commercial projects such as a listed museum, a pharmaceutical facility and a large community centre project. I had to leave this job at the inset of the recession in Ireland, and moved about (both Geographically and in terms of job direction) whilst trying to find a long term career. After a total of 5 years in a number of construction industry jobs, I decided I had given it a fair do, and as I had still not found something concrete, I took on a new challenge in Software development. I still look for small, side projects involving CAD drawing so that I can maintain my technical drawing skills and it still brings me satisfaction completing drawing tasks.
